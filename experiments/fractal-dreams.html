Fractal Dreams

Gently evolving Julia set fractals bathed in DMT-inspired colors. Pinch to zoom infinitely with a warm Shepard tone that rises as you dive deeper. Drag two fingers to pan. One finger warps the shape.

How to interact

📱 One finger: Drag across the screen to morph the fractal's shape. The colors and structure follow your touch.

🤏 Pinch out: Zoom out to see the bigger picture. Pinch in: Zoom into infinite detail — the deeper you go, the more you find.

✌🏼 Two fingers drag: Pan around while zoomed to explore different areas of the fractal.

🔊 Sound: A warm Shepard tone plays as you zoom — it rises when you zoom in and falls when you zoom out, matching the feeling of diving deeper or pulling back. The faster you pinch, the more you hear. It fades to silence when you stop.

🖱️ Desktop: Scroll to zoom, move your mouse to warp the shape. Audio starts on first scroll.

Interpretation

Fractals are the geometry of nature — found in ferns, river deltas, lightning bolts, and, some would say, the fabric of consciousness itself. The Julia set is a particular family of fractals where a simple mathematical rule, repeated endlessly, produces structures of staggering complexity. This piece lets that rule drift slowly over time, so the fractal morphs through an endless sequence of forms — each one a frozen moment from an infinite landscape. The DMT-inspired palette of deep indigos, electric magentas, and shimmering golds mirrors the visual language often reported in breakthrough psychedelic experiences: geometric, self-similar, and alive with color.

How it's done

A "Julia set" works by taking a point on screen and applying a simple rule over and over: square it, add a fixed number, repeat. Depending on the starting point, this either spirals away to infinity or stays bounded — and the boundary between those two behaviors is the fractal. The "fixed number" (called the Julia constant) determines the shape. In this experiment, that constant slowly drifts in a circle, which is why the fractal morphs continuously. Each pixel is colored based on how many iterations it took to escape — and those iteration counts are mapped to colors from a DMT-inspired palette. Pinch to zoom in and you'll discover ever-finer detail — fractals are literally infinite, so there's always more to see. The zoom uses an adaptive easing function that feels like moving through honey — fast when you're far, gentle as you arrive. A Shepard tone plays while you zoom: it's a clever audio illusion where layered tones constantly rise (or fall) but never seem to reach an end, perfectly mirroring the infinite zoom. Zoom in and the tone ascends; zoom out and it descends. The faster you pinch, the more you hear. Drag with two fingers to pan around. One finger still warps the Julia constant, changing the shape itself. All of this runs on your GPU as a fragment shader at 60 fps, with audio generated in real time by the Web Audio API.
